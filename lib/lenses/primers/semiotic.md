# Visual Semiotic / Semantic lens

## Definition

The visual semiotic lens reads the meme as a sign system: image as signifier, caption as signifier, their juxtaposition as the work. It takes seriously that memes are *radically intertextual* — half of what they mean is inherited from elsewhere (templates, reaction images, cultural shorthand, in-jokes that index whole subcultures). The lens unpacks the layering without flattening it. Where the historical lens asks *what is this meme*, the semiotic lens asks *how does this meme mean what it means*.

## Essential moves

- Distinguish **image** and **text** as separate sign systems, then read the **anchorage / relay** between them (Barthes: anchorage fixes the floating signifier of the image; relay lets image and text say different things in counterpoint).
- Identify **encoded cultural references** — the meme's intertexts. A meme analysis that doesn't name the references it borrows from is incomplete.
- Read **Peircean sign-type**: where is the meme functioning as **icon** (resemblance), **index** (causal trace), **symbol** (convention)? Most memes work all three at once.
- Fold in **Shifman's stance triad** — *participation structure* (who is the meme talking to), *keying* (serious / ironic / parodic), *communicative function* (information / persuasion / identity).
- Name what's specifically **vernacular** about the visual grammar — meme-native moves like the cropped-screenshot frame, the deep-fried filter, the impact-font caption, the reply-as-caption.
- Where image and text contradict, read the **contradiction as the meaning** (this is most reaction-meme work).
- Avoid the trap of describing the meme literally; describe what the *signs* are doing.

## Key theorists and short illustrative quotes

- **Roland Barthes**, "The Rhetoric of the Image" (in *Image-Music-Text*, 1977): on the linguistic message's two functions vis-à-vis the iconic message — *anchorage* ("[fixing] the floating chain of signifieds") and *relay* (text and image as complementary fragments of a single utterance).
- **Roland Barthes**, *Mythologies* (1957): on second-order signification — every image carries a denotation and, layered atop it, a myth.
- **Charles Sanders Peirce**, *Collected Papers*: the icon/index/symbol triad — useful for asking what *kind* of sign a given meme is operating as at a given moment.
- **Gunther Kress & Theo van Leeuwen**, *Reading Images: The Grammar of Visual Design* (1996): the most usable framework for systematic visual analysis (information value of placement, salience, framing).
- **Limor Shifman**, *Memes in Digital Culture* (2014): content / form / stance.

## Worked example openings (cathedral voice)

- "The image is iconic; the caption is symbolic; their collision is indexical of a class joke this audience has been making for a decade."
- "Anchorage here is doing almost no work — the caption tells you only what to feel, not what to see — and the relay between Mary and the Javelin is left wide open, which is the entire point."
- "Read denotatively, this is a stock-photo couple. Read mythologically, it is a structural map of every infidelity the internet has narrativized since 2017."

## Core corpus (priority)

## Mythologies

**Mythologies** is a 1957 collection of essays by French theorist Roland Barthes. Blending cultural criticism and semiotics, the book dissects everyday objects and media—from wrestling matches to advertisements—to reveal the ideological “myths” embedded in modern consumer culture. It remains a foundational text in 20th-century critical theory and cultural studies.

### Key facts

* **Author:** Roland Barthes
* **Original publication:** 1957 (France)
* **English translation:** 1972 (Annette Lavers); expanded 2013 by Richard Howard
* **Publisher:** Seuil (French original); Hill and Wang (English)
* **Genre:** Cultural criticism, semiotics

### Structure and themes

The book comprises two parts: a series of short essays analyzing aspects of post-war French life, and a concluding theoretical essay, “Myth Today.” In the first, Barthes interprets subjects such as professional wrestling, detergents, and the Citroën DS, showing how mass culture transforms history into nature—presenting social conventions as universal truths. “Myth Today” formalizes this analysis into a model of myth as a secondary semiological system built upon language.

### Cultural and intellectual impact

**Mythologies** introduced semiotic methods to popular culture studies and shaped disciplines from media theory to anthropology. Its approach influenced later thinkers including Jean Baudrillard and Stuart Hall. The work’s exposure of ideological naturalization remains a key analytical tool for decoding political messaging and advertising in contemporary society.

### Editions and translations

English editions include the influential 1972 translation by Annette Lavers and the complete 2013 version translated by Richard Howard and Annette Lavers, restoring essays omitted from earlier editions. The latter, published by Hill and Wang, emphasizes the book’s continuing relevance to digital and globalized media culture([Macmillan Publishers][1]).

## Course in General Linguistics

*Course in General Linguistics* is a foundational 1916 work in modern linguistics compiled from lectures by Swiss linguist Ferdinand de Saussure. Published posthumously by his students Charles Bally and Albert Sechehaye, it introduced key principles that reshaped language study and influenced the development of structuralism and semiotics across the humanities.

### Key facts

* **First publication:** 1916 (Payot, Lausanne and Paris)
* **Compiled by:** Charles Bally and Albert Sechehaye
* **Major English translations:** Wade Baskin (1959, Columbia University Press, 2011 ed.); Roy Harris (1983, Bloomsbury Academic, 2013 ed.)
* **Core concepts:** linguistic sign, signifier/signified, langue/parole, synchronic/diachronic analysis
* **Length:** about 320 pages (varies by edition)

### Background and publication

Saussure taught general linguistics at the University of Geneva from 1907 to 1911. After his death, notes taken by students were edited into *Cours de linguistique générale*. The resulting text unified disparate linguistic approaches and laid the groundwork for a scientific study of language as a structured system. Its posthumous compilation makes it both a record of lectures and a reconstruction of Saussure’s theoretical system. ([Columbia University Press][1])

### Core ideas

The book’s central insight is that language is a system of signs in which each sign unites a *signifier* (sound pattern) and a *signified* (concept). Meaning arises from differences between signs, not from inherent reference to the world. Saussure distinguishes *langue* (the structured system of a language) from *parole* (individual speech acts), and promotes a *synchronic* approach to studying language structure alongside the traditional *diachronic* study of change. ([Columbia University Press][1])

### Influence and legacy

*Course in General Linguistics* profoundly shaped 20th-century thought. It underpins structural linguistics and influenced scholars such as Claude Lévi-Strauss, Roland Barthes, Jacques Lacan, and Jacques Derrida. The concepts of structure and sign inspired fields from anthropology to literary theory, giving rise to semiotics, structuralism, and later post-structuralist critiques. ([Bloomsbury][2])

### Editions and accessibility

Modern readers commonly use the Bloomsbury Revelations edition translated by Roy Harris (2013) or Columbia University Press’s critical edition edited by Perry Meisel and Haun Saussy (2011). Both include contextual introductions and annotations situating Saussure within the history of ideas. ([Bloomsbury][3])
