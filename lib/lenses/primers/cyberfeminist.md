# Cyberfeminist lens

## Definition

The cyberfeminist lens reads the meme through the politics of embodiment, technology, and gender online. It asks: who is the gendered subject of this image, whose body is visible / invisible / weaponized, what kind of feminized labor (emotional, identity-curatorial, care-coded, affect-managerial) is being performed, and what does the network do to the body it represents? The lens treats meme-making itself as a feminized labor form — unpaid identity-curation that produces value others capture — and meme-circulation as a feminized political form, the meme-as-care-work, mutual aid coordinated through humor.

## Essential moves

- Locate **the gendered subject**: who is depicted, who is implied as speaker, who is implied as audience.
- Read **visibility / invisibility** structurally — whose body is on screen and whose body is doing the unpaid work *behind* the screen (identity-curation, moderation, support).
- Name the **feminized labor form**: identity-curation, affect-management, the meme-as-emotional-housekeeping for a public.
- Track the **cyborg figure** wherever it appears — partial, networked, infidel to origins (Haraway).
- Apply the **glitch** as method (Russell): the breakdown that refuses smooth function as a politics rather than a failure.
- For military / religious overlays (Saint Javelin et al.): name the **Marian-icon-as-weapon** move directly — the cyberfeminist + necropolitical intersection is the whole reading.
- Distinguish **representation** (what is depicted) from **infrastructure** (who is shaping the network the meme moves through). Both are gendered; they are not gendered the same way.

## Key theorists and short illustrative quotes

- **Donna Haraway**, "A Cyborg Manifesto" (1985): "The cyborg is a creature in a post-gender world; it has no truck with bisexuality, pre-oedipal symbiosis, unalienated labour, or other seductions to organic wholeness through a final appropriation of all the powers of the parts into a higher unity." The cyborg is the figure of partial, networked, *infidel* subjectivity.
- **Legacy Russell**, *Glitch Feminism: A Manifesto* (Verso, 2020): "We are the most fertile when we ourselves are a mess." The body online is already-glitched; the glitch is method.
- **Sadie Plant**, *Zeros + Ones* (1997): on the gendered history of computation and the always-already-feminized labor of the network.
- **Silvia Federici**, *Caliban and the Witch* (2004): historical baseline on reproductive and affective labor, ties cyberfem back to Marxist-feminist roots.
- **Laboria Cuboniks** / **Helen Hester**, on xenofeminism — a more accelerationist register: "if nature is unjust, change nature."

## Worked example openings (cathedral voice)

- "Saint Javelin works because the Marian icon — the most feminized image in the Christian repertoire — is asked to hold an anti-tank weapon, and the icon holds it without strain."
- "The unpaid labor here is not the meme's making but its circulation: the women who passed this image into the right group chats so it would arrive in front of the right eyes."
- "Whoever drew this dog is invisible; whatever woman runs the account that hosts it does the work of keeping the dog alive; the platform takes the difference."

## A Cyborg Manifesto

*A Cyborg Manifesto: Science, Technology, and Socialist-Feminism in the Late Twentieth Century* is a landmark 1985 essay by American scholar Donna Haraway. Blending feminist theory, Marxism, and science and technology studies, it uses the figure of the “cyborg”—a hybrid of machine and organism—to critique essentialist ideas of gender and reimagine politics in a technoscientific world.

### Key facts

* **Author:** Donna J. Haraway
* **First published:** 1985 (*Socialist Review*); revised 1991 (in *Simians, Cyborgs, and Women*)
* **Genre:** Feminist theory / science and technology studies
* **Famous line:** “I would rather be a cyborg than a goddess.”
* **Core metaphor:** Cyborg as hybrid subject disrupting boundaries of nature, gender, and machine

### Context and composition

Haraway wrote the manifesto amid 1980s debates on feminism, Marxism, and high technology. It first appeared as “Manifesto for Cyborgs” in *Socialist Review* and was revised for inclusion in her 1991 book *Simians, Cyborgs, and Women* (Routledge). The essay responds to Cold War militarism, the rise of information capitalism, and internal feminist disputes over identity and essentialism.

### Main ideas

The manifesto argues that late twentieth-century technoscience collapses three traditional boundaries: **human/animal**, **organism/machine**, and **physical/non-physical**. These breakdowns expose the artificiality of social hierarchies and open possibilities for new political identities. Rejecting the notion of a universal feminine essence, Haraway proposes “**affinity politics**”—coalitions formed through partial, chosen alliances rather than shared biology or experience. The “cyborg” becomes both a critical and imaginative figure for such politics, symbolizing hybrid, networked, and situated subjectivities.

### Style and method

Haraway presents the work as an **“ironic political myth.”** Mixing academic theory, science fiction imagery, and humor, she performs the hybridity she describes. The manifesto’s nonlinear, allusive style resists systematic exposition, encouraging readers to think experimentally about technology, identity, and power.

### Influence and legacy

*A Cyborg Manifesto* helped inaugurate **cyberfeminism** and influenced **posthumanism**, **queer theory**, and **science and technology studies**. Its concepts—especially the “informatics of domination” and the critique of essentialism—remain central to analyses of digital culture, biotechnology, and intersectional politics. The essay’s enduring provocation, “rather cyborg than goddess,” continues to challenge assumptions about nature, gender, and the politics of technological life. ([The Ted K Archive][1])

## Testo Junkie

*Testo Junkie: Sex, Drugs, and Biopolitics in the Pharmacopornographic Era* is a 2013 book by philosopher and curator Paul B. Preciado, first published in Spanish in 2008 and translated into English by Bruce Benderson. Blending memoir and theory, it examines how hormones, pharmaceuticals, and pornography shape gender and sexuality in what Preciado calls the “pharmacopornographic” era.

### Key facts

* **Author:** Paul B. Preciado
* **Translator:** Bruce Benderson
* **Original publication:** 2008 (Spanish)
* **English edition:** Feminist Press at CUNY, 2013
* **ISBN:** 978-1-55861-837-4

### Concept and structure

The book merges critical theory with personal diary. Preciado documents a yearlong self-administration of testosterone gel while developing a political and philosophical critique of biopower. Through this experiment, he links the molecular management of bodies to capitalism’s production of desire, arguing that sex and gender are regulated through overlapping pharmaceutical and pornographic technologies.

### The “pharmacopornographic era”

Preciado’s term describes a social regime in which hormones, drugs, and media images govern sexual subjectivity. Drawing on Michel Foucault’s *The History of Sexuality*, the author traces how post-1950s industries—from birth control to Viagra—transform bodies and identities into biotechnological commodities. This analysis situates gender as a mutable construct produced by pharmacological and visual economies.

### Style and reception

Part philosophical treatise and part confessional narrative, *Testo Junkie* is noted for its hybrid style reminiscent of William S. Burroughs and Kathy Acker. Critics have praised its theoretical ambition and poetic intensity, while some note its limited engagement with transfeminine experiences. It has become a foundational text in contemporary queer, trans, and feminist theory, influencing debates on embodiment, autonomy, and biopolitics.
