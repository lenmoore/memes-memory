# Cyberfeminist lens

## Definition

The cyberfeminist lens reads the meme through the politics of embodiment, technology, and gender online. It asks: who is the gendered subject of this image, whose body is visible / invisible / weaponized, what kind of feminized labor (emotional, identity-curatorial, care-coded, affect-managerial) is being performed, and what does the network do to the body it represents? The lens treats meme-making itself as a feminized labor form — unpaid identity-curation that produces value others capture — and meme-circulation as a feminized political form, the meme-as-care-work, mutual aid coordinated through humor.

## Essential moves

- Locate **the gendered subject**: who is depicted, who is implied as speaker, who is implied as audience.
- Read **visibility / invisibility** structurally — whose body is on screen and whose body is doing the unpaid work *behind* the screen (identity-curation, moderation, support).
- Name the **feminized labor form**: identity-curation, affect-management, the meme-as-emotional-housekeeping for a public.
- Track the **cyborg figure** wherever it appears — partial, networked, infidel to origins (Haraway).
- Apply the **glitch** as method (Russell): the breakdown that refuses smooth function as a politics rather than a failure.
- For military / religious overlays (Saint Javelin et al.): name the **Marian-icon-as-weapon** move directly — the cyberfeminist + necropolitical intersection is the whole reading.
- Distinguish **representation** (what is depicted) from **infrastructure** (who is shaping the network the meme moves through). Both are gendered; they are not gendered the same way.

## Key theorists and short illustrative quotes

- **Donna Haraway**, "A Cyborg Manifesto" (1985): "The cyborg is a creature in a post-gender world; it has no truck with bisexuality, pre-oedipal symbiosis, unalienated labour, or other seductions to organic wholeness through a final appropriation of all the powers of the parts into a higher unity." The cyborg is the figure of partial, networked, *infidel* subjectivity.
- **Legacy Russell**, *Glitch Feminism: A Manifesto* (Verso, 2020): "We are the most fertile when we ourselves are a mess." The body online is already-glitched; the glitch is method.
- **Sadie Plant**, *Zeros + Ones* (1997): on the gendered history of computation and the always-already-feminized labor of the network.
- **Silvia Federici**, *Caliban and the Witch* (2004): historical baseline on reproductive and affective labor, ties cyberfem back to Marxist-feminist roots.
- **Laboria Cuboniks** / **Helen Hester**, on xenofeminism — a more accelerationist register: "if nature is unjust, change nature."

## Worked example openings (cathedral voice)

- "Saint Javelin works because the Marian icon — the most feminized image in the Christian repertoire — is asked to hold an anti-tank weapon, and the icon holds it without strain."
- "The unpaid labor here is not the meme's making but its circulation: the women who passed this image into the right group chats so it would arrive in front of the right eyes."
- "Whoever drew this dog is invisible; whatever woman runs the account that hosts it does the work of keeping the dog alive; the platform takes the difference."
