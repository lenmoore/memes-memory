# Sociological lens

## Definition

The sociological lens reads the meme as identity work. It asks: what is the sharer signalling about themselves by circulating this, what group are they performing membership in, what social position becomes legible in the act of sharing, and what does the meme cost (in time, taste, risk) such that it functions as a *signal*. Sharing a meme is, in Erving Goffman's vocabulary, impression management — a low-cost, high-information performance of who you are and who you're with. Where the anthropological lens reads kinship, the sociological lens reads stratification.

## Essential moves

- Apply **Goffmanian dramaturgy**: the meme is a performance, the timeline is the front stage, the group chat is the back stage. Name the role being performed.
- Read **frame** (Goffman, *Frame Analysis*): what definition-of-the-situation is the meme invoking, and against what alternative frame.
- Treat **context collapse** (boyd) as the default condition: a meme means different things simultaneously to coworkers, family, exes, employers, and a public it cannot see. Name what each constituency reads.
- Identify the **signalling cost** (Spence-style): a meme that's free for everyone signals nothing. A meme that costs taste or risk to share is a legible signal of who can afford it.
- Treat **taste as class** (Bourdieu): the meme's aesthetic is a position in a field; circulation enacts a habitus. Name the habitus.
- Read **networked-public visibility** as a structural condition, not an option (boyd): the audience is invisible, persistent, searchable, and replicable.
- Distinguish **identity expression** (this is me) from **identity declaration** (I am the kind of person who shares this).

## Key theorists and short illustrative quotes

- **Erving Goffman**, *The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life* (1956): "When an individual plays a part he implicitly requests his observers to take seriously the impression that is fostered before them." Sharing a meme is a small such request.
- **Erving Goffman**, *Frame Analysis* (1974): on the frames within which definitions of situations are organized — the meme is almost always a frame-invocation.
- **danah boyd**, *It's Complicated* (2014): networked publics have four affordances — *persistence, searchability, replicability, scalability* — and four dynamics — *invisible audiences, context collapse, the blurring of public and private, the necessity of performing for an unknown crowd*.
- **Pierre Bourdieu**, *Distinction* (1979): "Taste classifies, and it classifies the classifier." A meme's aesthetic is a small classification operation.
- **Michael Spence**, "Job Market Signaling" (1973): signals work only when they are differentially costly.

## Worked example openings (cathedral voice)

- "To share this meme in 2024 is to declare which of three mutually unreadable subcultures one belongs to; the cost of being wrong is being legible to the wrong people."
- "The performance here is not the meme but the *timing* of the share — early enough to look like vanguard taste, late enough to look like consensus."
- "Bourdieu's small revenge: a class position made visible in a single ironic image, whose recipients will recognize the position whether or not the sharer means them to."
