# Geopolitical / Postcolonial / Military lens

## Definition

The geopolitical lens reads the meme as embedded in conflict, asymmetric power, and the political economy of attention across a global media order. It asks: whose death is grievable here, whose is administrable, who is being made legible as a person and who is being made legible as data, and how does this particular meme participate in that distribution? Diasporic memes are constitutively geopolitical — they are the form in which displaced people narrate their displacement to themselves and to a world audience that may or may not be listening.

This is a combined lens (geopolitical + postcolonial + military) because for the project's diasporic corpus the three overlap so heavily that splitting them weakens the reading. Three thin lenses lose what one fat one can hold.

## Essential moves

- Identify the **conflict** the meme is embedded in — direct, mediated, or atmospheric. Do not treat memes as politically neutral by default; their distribution is the politics.
- Ask **whose grief is being made legible** and whose is not (Butler, *Frames of War* / *Precarious Life*) — meme circulation is a distribution mechanism for grievability.
- Read the **necropolitical sorting** (Mbembe): who gets framed as living-and-can-die, who as already-dying, who as administrable corpse.
- Where Orientalist or colonial framings are operative, name them (Said) — including in supposedly anti-Orientalist memes that reproduce the framing they critique.
- For war memes specifically, attend to **the militarization of perception** (Virilio): the meme as munition, as morale instrument, as forensic evidence.
- Ask **who is speaking through whom** (Spivak): is the subaltern in this meme allowed to speak, or only allowed to be spoken about by sympathetic Western intermediaries?
- For evidence-bearing memes (SUCHO material, OSINT), Eyal Weizman's **forensic** register applies — the meme as counter-archive.

## Key theorists and short illustrative quotes

- **Achille Mbembe**, "Necropolitics" (2003): "to exercise sovereignty is to exercise control over mortality and to define life as the deployment and manifestation of power." The question is not only who lives but who is allowed to die meaningfully.
- **Judith Butler**, *Frames of War* (2009): on the differential allocation of *grievability* — whose life is recognized as life such that its loss can register as loss.
- **Edward Said**, *Orientalism* (1978): on the discursive production of "the East" as the West's intelligible other.
- **Paul Virilio**, *War and Cinema* (1984): on the militarization of perception; the camera and the gun share an optical lineage. Memes inherit this.
- **Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak**, "Can the Subaltern Speak?" (1988): the impossibility of unmediated subaltern testimony in the dominant register.
- **Eyal Weizman**, *Forensic Architecture* (2017): the image as evidentiary, not only rhetorical.

## Worked example openings (cathedral voice)

- "The meme works because it asks a Western audience to grieve a Ukrainian death the same audience would not be asked to grieve in any other key — and because the audience does."
- "What Said called Orientalism is here repurposed as protest: the meme borrows the framing of the colonial gaze in order to throw it back at the imperial state it depicts."
- "Every NAFO fella is a small instrument of perceptual war; the Shiba is the optic through which a state's lies become visibly absurd."
