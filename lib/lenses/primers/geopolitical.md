# Geopolitical / Postcolonial / Military lens

## Definition

The geopolitical lens reads the meme as embedded in conflict, asymmetric power, and the political economy of attention across a global media order. It asks: whose death is grievable here, whose is administrable, who is being made legible as a person and who is being made legible as data, and how does this particular meme participate in that distribution? Diasporic memes are constitutively geopolitical — they are the form in which displaced people narrate their displacement to themselves and to a world audience that may or may not be listening.

This is a combined lens (geopolitical + postcolonial + military) because for the project's diasporic corpus the three overlap so heavily that splitting them weakens the reading. Three thin lenses lose what one fat one can hold.

## Essential moves

- Identify the **conflict** the meme is embedded in — direct, mediated, or atmospheric. Do not treat memes as politically neutral by default; their distribution is the politics.
- Ask **whose grief is being made legible** and whose is not (Butler, *Frames of War* / *Precarious Life*) — meme circulation is a distribution mechanism for grievability.
- Read the **necropolitical sorting** (Mbembe): who gets framed as living-and-can-die, who as already-dying, who as administrable corpse.
- Where Orientalist or colonial framings are operative, name them (Said) — including in supposedly anti-Orientalist memes that reproduce the framing they critique.
- For war memes specifically, attend to **the militarization of perception** (Virilio): the meme as munition, as morale instrument, as forensic evidence.
- Ask **who is speaking through whom** (Spivak): is the subaltern in this meme allowed to speak, or only allowed to be spoken about by sympathetic Western intermediaries?
- For evidence-bearing memes (SUCHO material, OSINT), Eyal Weizman's **forensic** register applies — the meme as counter-archive.

## Key theorists and short illustrative quotes

- **Achille Mbembe**, "Necropolitics" (2003): "to exercise sovereignty is to exercise control over mortality and to define life as the deployment and manifestation of power." The question is not only who lives but who is allowed to die meaningfully.
- **Judith Butler**, *Frames of War* (2009): on the differential allocation of *grievability* — whose life is recognized as life such that its loss can register as loss.
- **Edward Said**, *Orientalism* (1978): on the discursive production of "the East" as the West's intelligible other.
- **Paul Virilio**, *War and Cinema* (1984): on the militarization of perception; the camera and the gun share an optical lineage. Memes inherit this.
- **Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak**, "Can the Subaltern Speak?" (1988): the impossibility of unmediated subaltern testimony in the dominant register.
- **Eyal Weizman**, *Forensic Architecture* (2017): the image as evidentiary, not only rhetorical.

## Worked example openings (cathedral voice)

- "The meme works because it asks a Western audience to grieve a Ukrainian death the same audience would not be asked to grieve in any other key — and because the audience does."
- "What Said called Orientalism is here repurposed as protest: the meme borrows the framing of the colonial gaze in order to throw it back at the imperial state it depicts."
- "Every NAFO fella is a small instrument of perceptual war; the Shiba is the optic through which a state's lies become visibly absurd."

## The Revenge of Geography

*The Revenge of Geography: What the Map Tells Us About Coming Conflicts and the Battle Against Fate* is a 2012 nonfiction book by American foreign affairs analyst **Robert D. Kaplan**. It argues that geography—mountains, rivers, deserts, and resources—continues to shape global politics and constrain human ambitions despite technological advances and ideology.

### Key facts

* **Author:** Robert D. Kaplan
* **Published:** 2012 (Random House)
* **Genre:** Geopolitics, international relations
* **Central theme:** Enduring influence of geography on global power
* **Notable idea:** “Geography is destiny” reinterpreted for the 21st century

### Geographic determinism and its legacy

Kaplan reintroduces classical geopolitics to contemporary readers by reviving the work of early theorists such as **Halford Mackinder**, **Nicholas Spykman**, and **Alfred Thayer Mahan**. He contends that geography—location, terrain, and natural barriers—remains the most reliable predictor of national behavior. The book cautions policymakers against ignoring physical constraints in favor of purely ideological or economic explanations.

### Structure and scope

Kaplan divides the narrative by regions: Europe, the Middle East, South Asia, East Asia, and the Americas. Each section traces how landscapes influence state formation, borders, and strategic rivalries. For example, he links Russia’s vast plains to its insecurity, China’s coastal geography to its maritime ambitions, and the Middle East’s deserts to enduring fragmentation.

### Critical reception and influence

The book received mixed but substantial attention in policy and academic circles. Admirers praised its synthesis of history and geography in explaining current conflicts; critics saw it as deterministic, overlooking culture and agency. It has nonetheless become a common reference in strategic studies, influencing debates about power competition in Eurasia and the limits of globalization.

### Continuing relevance

A decade after publication, *The Revenge of Geography* remains cited in discussions of U.S.–China rivalry, Russia’s regional assertiveness, and the persistence of geopolitical constraints in an era of climate change and technological connectivity.

## Imagined Communities

*Imagined Communities: Reflections on the Origin and Spread of Nationalism* is a 1983 book by political scientist **Benedict Anderson**. It is a foundational text in the study of nationalism, arguing that nations are socially constructed “imagined communities” formed through shared media, language, and historical narratives rather than inherent ethnic or territorial bonds.

### Key facts

* **Author:** Benedict Anderson
* **First published:** 1983 (Verso Books)
* **Subject:** Nationalism and identity formation
* **Influence:** Major work in political science, sociology, and cultural studies
* **Updated edition:** 1991, with expanded discussion of nationalism’s global evolution

### Concept and argument

Anderson’s central thesis is that a nation exists because people imagine themselves as part of a larger community of fellow members, most of whom they will never meet. This sense of unity, he argues, arose historically from cultural and technological shifts—especially print capitalism—that standardized languages and fostered collective consciousness. The book emphasizes that nationalism is a modern, historically contingent phenomenon rather than a natural expression of human identity.

### Historical and theoretical context

Drawing on examples from Europe, the Americas, and Southeast Asia, Anderson situates nationalism within the decline of dynastic empires and the rise of secular, print-based societies. His analysis challenges earlier views that saw nations as primordial or inevitable, linking national identity instead to political modernization, colonial experiences, and the spread of vernacular languages.

### Reception and legacy

*Imagined Communities* has been widely cited across disciplines, influencing scholars of history, anthropology, and literary studies. Its concepts of imagined identity and media’s role in community formation remain central to debates on globalization, transnationalism, and digital cultures. The book is considered one of the most important theoretical contributions to understanding nationalism in the late 20th century.

## Can the Subaltern Speak?

**Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak’s *Can the Subaltern Speak?*** (1983–1988) is a seminal essay in postcolonial and feminist theory. It interrogates whether those most marginalized by colonialism, class, caste, and gender—“the subaltern”—can ever truly be heard within dominant structures of knowledge and power. First appearing in *Marxism and the Interpretation of Culture*, it remains one of the most cited works in global cultural studies.

### Key facts

* **Author:** Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak
* **Written:** 1983–1987; canonical 1988 version (University of Illinois Press)
* **Genre:** Postcolonial and feminist critical theory
* **Core question:** Can the subaltern achieve legible self-representation?
* **Signature concept:** “The subaltern cannot speak”

### Context and structure

Emerging amid debates in Marxism, poststructuralism, and decolonization, Spivak’s essay engages thinkers such as Michel Foucault, Gilles Deleuze, and the Subaltern Studies Collective. It critiques both Western radical theory and Indian nationalist historiography for overlooking how power mediates every act of “speaking for” the oppressed. Spivak distinguishes *Vertretung* (political representation) from *Darstellung* (discursive depiction) to show that intellectuals inevitably participate in the structures they critique.

### Central arguments

The essay’s most famous claim—“The subaltern cannot speak”—does not denote literal silence but a structural condition: within hegemonic discourses, the subaltern’s voice cannot be recognized as authoritative or intelligible. Spivak develops this through her analysis of colonial debates on *sati* (widow immolation) and the 1926 suicide of Bhubaneswari Bhaduri, illustrating how colonial and patriarchal narratives erase women’s agency. These readings exemplify her notion of **epistemic violence**, the harm done when dominant knowledge systems pre-structure marginalized experience.

### Method and influence

Combining deconstruction with postcolonial feminism, Spivak advances a self-reflexive critique of intellectual complicity and advocates **strategic essentialism**—temporary, conscious solidarity among marginalized groups. The essay has profoundly influenced feminist theory, historiography, anthropology, and cultural studies, shaping debates on representation, archival silence, and global South epistemologies.

### Legacy

Revised versions appear in Spivak’s *A Critique of Postcolonial Reason* and in *Can the Subaltern Speak? Reflections on the History of an Idea* (ed. Rosalind C. Morris). Its enduring relevance lies in insisting that questions of voice and power remain central to any decolonial or feminist practice of knowledge.

## On War

*On War* (original title: *Vom Kriege*) is a military theory treatise written by Prussian general and strategist **Carl von Clausewitz**. Published posthumously in 1832, it remains one of the most influential works on the philosophy, strategy, and nature of war, shaping both military doctrine and political thought worldwide.

### Key facts

* **Author:** Carl von Clausewitz
* **First published:** 1832 (posthumously)
* **Original language:** German (*Vom Kriege*)
* **Subject:** Military theory and strategy
* **Core concept:** “War as a continuation of politics by other means”

### Background

Clausewitz wrote *On War* after serving in the Napoleonic Wars, intending to provide a comprehensive analysis of warfare’s logic and psychology. His unfinished manuscript was edited and published by his wife, Marie von Clausewitz, following his death. The book emerged during a time of shifting European power and professional military reform.

### Core ideas

At its center is the proposition that war is not an isolated act but part of political intercourse, where violence serves political objectives. Clausewitz distinguishes between the “absolute” or “ideal” war—a theoretical construct of total violence—and real war, limited by friction, uncertainty, and human factors. He emphasizes the “trinity” of passion (people), chance (army/commander), and reason (government).

### Influence and legacy

*On War* became foundational for modern strategic thought, influencing figures from **Otto von Bismarck** to **Henry Kissinger**. It guided military education in Europe and the United States and has been applied to political science, international relations, and organizational strategy. Despite its dense prose, its core insights remain central to debates about the nature and purpose of conflict.

### Modern relevance

Contemporary scholars interpret Clausewitz in contexts ranging from nuclear deterrence to asymmetric warfare. His insistence on understanding war’s political nature continues to influence strategic thinking in the 21st century, ensuring *On War* endures as a living document in military and political studies.
