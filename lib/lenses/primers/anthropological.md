# Anthropological lens

## Definition

The anthropological lens reads the meme as a ritual object circulating in a kinship structure. It asks: who is bound to whom by sharing this, what is being exchanged when it is shared, what passage or boundary is being marked, what reciprocal obligation is incurred? A meme is, in Marcel Mauss's sense, almost exactly a *gift* — circulated to make and maintain bonds, carrying obligations of return (you laughed at mine, you owe me a laugh at yours), generating prestige through display. The familiar "you get it or you don't" structure of memes is, in the lens's vocabulary, a kinship-marker function.

## Essential moves

- Read the meme as **gift** (Mauss): what is given, what return is implied, what prestige accrues to the giver.
- Identify **kinship boundary** the meme draws — who is inside the joke, who is outside, what does crossing the line cost.
- Apply **liminality / communitas** (Turner): memes thrive in the between-state and create temporary communities that dissolve on closer contact. Name the liminal threshold this one is operating across.
- Use **purity / danger** (Douglas) for memes that work by transgressing categories — most memes do, by mixing registers (sacred / profane, formal / vernacular, high / low).
- Note **rites of passage** (van Gennep / Turner): some memes mark transitions (graduation, first child, divorce, citizenship) and the lens should name which.
- Trace the **circulation pattern** — group chat to platform to public to archive — as a kinship topology, not a distribution channel.
- Where **cultural capital** (Bourdieu) is doing the work, name the field: knowing this meme is the capital, and the field marks who counts.

## Key theorists and short illustrative quotes

- **Marcel Mauss**, *The Gift* (1925): "Apparently free and disinterested, the gift is nevertheless constrained and interested." Every meme shared imposes a debt.
- **Victor Turner**, *The Ritual Process* (1969): on *communitas* — the unstructured equality that forms within liminal moments and dissolves outside them. Group-chat communities are textbook communitas.
- **Mary Douglas**, *Purity and Danger* (1966): dirt as "matter out of place." Transgressive memes are, exactly, matter out of place — the Madonna with an anti-tank weapon is *displaced* matter that becomes powerful.
- **Claude Lévi-Strauss**, *The Elementary Structures of Kinship* (1949): the formal mathematics of who-can-share-with-whom.
- **Pierre Bourdieu**, *Distinction* (1979): taste as cultural capital. To recognize this meme is to display class position.

## Worked example openings (cathedral voice)

- "The meme is a gift. The recipient owes one back, in kind, within forty-eight hours, or the bond it offered is forfeit."
- "What Turner called communitas is exactly what a group chat performs at three in the morning; this image is the relic of that night's communitas, posted public so the rest of us can mourn that we weren't there."
- "Saint Javelin is matter out of place — a Madonna where a weapons-systems poster should be — and in Mary Douglas's vocabulary, that displacement is the source of its sacred power, not a defect of its taste."

## The Interpretation of Cultures

**The Interpretation of Cultures** is a landmark 1973 collection of essays by American anthropologist Clifford Geertz. It redefined cultural anthropology by introducing the method of “thick description” and advancing an interpretive, symbol-based understanding of culture. The book remains foundational across the social sciences and humanities. ([Hachette Book Group][1])

### Key facts

* **Author:** Clifford Geertz
* **First published:** 1973 (Basic Books)
* **Length:** ~576 pages (2017 edition)
* **Genre:** Cultural anthropology / social theory
* **Notable concept:** “Thick description”

### Core themes and approach

Geertz argues that culture is not a determinant of behavior but a “web of meanings” humans have spun and inhabit. Understanding these symbolic webs requires contextual interpretation rather than universal laws. His interpretive turn contrasted with structuralist, functionalist, and positivist approaches dominant in mid-century anthropology. ([The Center for Cosmopolitan Culture][2])

### Essays and case studies

Among the book’s most famous pieces is “Deep Play: Notes on the Balinese Cockfight,” which demonstrates how ethnographic detail can reveal layers of social meaning, status, and emotion. Other essays explore religion, ideology, and systems of symbols across societies, blending literary sensibility with empirical observation.

### Influence and legacy

The volume catalyzed the rise of symbolic and interpretive anthropology, shaping adjacent disciplines such as sociology, history, and cultural studies. It was named by the *Times Literary Supplement* as one of the 100 most important books since World War II. Geertz’s method emphasized that anthropology is a humanistic science—its aim not prediction but understanding through interpretation. ([Hachette Book Group][1])

### Editions and publication

Originally issued by Basic Books, the work has appeared in multiple print, digital, and audiobook editions, including a 2017 release featuring a foreword by historian Robert Darnton. It continues to serve as a core text in anthropology and cultural theory worldwide. ([Hachette Book Group][1])

## The Sacred and the Profane

*The Sacred and the Profane: The Nature of Religion* is a 1957 work by Romanian historian of religion Mircea Eliade. The book explores how religious experience shapes human perception of reality, contrasting the sacred—spaces, times, and actions set apart and imbued with meaning—with the profane, the ordinary and secular. It remains a foundational text in the phenomenology of religion.

### Key facts

* **Author:** Mircea Eliade
* **Original publication:** 1957 (English translation, 1959)
* **Original language:** French (*Le Sacré et le Profane*)
* **Subject:** Comparative religion, sacred–profane dichotomy
* **Publisher:** Harcourt (English edition)

### Core thesis

Eliade argues that religious humans, or *homo religiosus*, experience the world through hierophanies—manifestations of the sacred that reveal a deeper, transcendent reality. These manifestations establish “centers” and “axes” around which the religious life is oriented. The profane, in contrast, is characterized by homogeneity and lacks existential depth.

### Structure and themes

The book is organized into four main chapters: sacred space, sacred time, sacred nature, and the sacredness of human existence. Each explores how religious symbolism transforms ordinary experience into a reflection of cosmic order. Eliade examines examples from archaic religions, mythologies, and ritual behaviors to show continuities in human efforts to locate meaning within the cosmos.

### Influence and reception

*The Sacred and the Profane* became one of Eliade’s most widely read works, influencing the academic study of religion, anthropology, and cultural theory. Its dichotomy between sacred and profane has been debated by later scholars, some arguing it oversimplifies cultural differences or neglects power dynamics in religious life. Despite critique, it remains a touchstone for understanding how humans construct sacred worlds.

### Legacy

Eliade’s framework inspired later studies of myth, symbolism, and ritual, informing thinkers from Joseph Campbell to contemporary phenomenologists of religion. The book continues to be used in university courses on comparative religion and cultural history for its lucid synthesis of Eliade’s central ideas.

## The Study of Folklore

*The Study of Folklore* is a 1965 academic anthology edited by American folklorist **Alan Dundes**. The volume compiles foundational essays defining and shaping folklore as a modern scholarly field, marking a major turning point in U.S. folklore studies by bringing together anthropological, literary, and psychological perspectives.

### Key facts

* **Editor:** Alan Dundes
* **Publication year:** 1965
* **Publisher:** Prentice-Hall
* **Genre:** Folklore scholarship / academic anthology
* **Notable contributors:** Stith Thompson, Richard M. Dorson, Dell Hymes

### Origins and purpose

Dundes assembled *The Study of Folklore* to provide a core set of readings for university courses in folklore. At the time, folklore study was scattered across disciplines; this collection unified diverse methodologies under a single academic framework. It emphasized folklore as a systematic, comparative study of traditional expressive culture rather than a mere catalog of curiosities.

### Content and contributors

The anthology includes classic essays by leading mid-20th-century folklorists on definitions, genres (such as myth, legend, and joke), and methods of analysis. Contributors debated central concepts like “folk group” and “folk idea,” reflecting shifts toward performance and context-based interpretations that would dominate later decades.

### Scholarly impact

*The Study of Folklore* became a standard teaching text and influenced the institutionalization of folklore programs across North America. Dundes’s editorial commentary and conceptual framing encouraged rigorous analytic and theoretical approaches, helping folklore gain recognition as an autonomous discipline within the social sciences and humanities.

### Legacy and later developments

The book’s influence persisted through updated companion volumes, notably *Folklore Matters* and *The Study of Folklore in Literature and Culture*, which extended its insights to literary and cultural studies. Today, the 1965 anthology remains a landmark reference for understanding how folklore emerged as a field of academic inquiry.

## Memes as contemporary folklore systems

For this lens, internet memes are read as contemporary folklore systems — vernacular expressive culture in Dundes's sense, requiring thick description in Geertz's sense.
