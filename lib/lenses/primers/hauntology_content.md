# Hauntology (Content) lens

## Definition

The hauntology lens reads the meme as a haunted object — an image inhabited by ghosts of older cultural forms, dead futures it carries without mourning, unmournable losses it makes momentarily speakable. It asks: what is being re-animated here, what does the act of re-animation reveal about what was killed, and what futures are present *only as absence*? Many memes are explicitly anachronistic — they pastiche Renaissance paintings, medieval marginalia, Soviet posters, 90s sitcoms, late-2000s flash animations. The hauntological reading takes that anachronism structurally rather than dismissively. (This is the *content* version of the lens — the ghosts inside the image. A separate medium-hauntology lens reads the platform itself as haunted.)

## Essential moves

- Identify the **anachronism** the meme is staging — what historical register is being summoned, and what work does the summoning do.
- Read the **lost future** the image is in mourning for, in Fisher's sense: not just nostalgia for a past but grief for a future that was promised and didn't arrive.
- Apply the **spectral logic of inheritance** (Derrida): every meme is in debt to images it cannot fully repay or refuse.
- Distinguish **restorative** from **reflective** nostalgia (Boym): does the meme want to *restore* the lost moment, or *dwell with* its loss?
- Notice when the meme is mourning something it cannot name — diasporic homelands, dead platforms, abandoned political projects, severed kinships.
- Use Benjamin's **angel of history** carefully — the past as wreckage piling at the angel's feet — for memes that explicitly archive disaster.
- Avoid the trap of treating "nostalgia" as the whole reading. Hauntology is the *structure* of nostalgia, not its synonym.

## Key theorists and short illustrative quotes

- **Mark Fisher**, *Ghosts of My Life* (Zero, 2014): "The slow cancellation of the future has been accompanied by a deflation of expectations." The meme is among the few cultural forms that still register that deflation.
- **Mark Fisher**, *Capitalist Realism* (2009): "It is easier to imagine the end of the world than the end of capitalism." Hauntological memes work in the gap that sentence opens.
- **Jacques Derrida**, *Specters of Marx* (1993): "A spectre is haunting Europe… One must always *go further* with this haunting." Hauntology proper begins here.
- **Svetlana Boym**, *The Future of Nostalgia* (2001): restorative nostalgia "stresses *nostos* and attempts a transhistorical reconstruction of the lost home"; reflective nostalgia "dwells on *algia*, on longing and loss." Diasporic memes are almost always the second kind, sometimes performing as the first.
- **Walter Benjamin**, "Theses on the Philosophy of History" (1940): the angel of history sees a single catastrophe piling wreckage upon wreckage.

## Worked example openings (cathedral voice)

- "The meme is haunted by an internet that no longer exists; its formal grammar — the bevelled edges, the gif compression, the Comic Sans — is a séance for that internet, conducted in front of an audience that does not realize a séance is what it is."
- "What is being mourned here is not the loss of a homeland but the loss of the future in which the homeland was supposed to become livable; Fisher's slow cancellation runs through every pixel."
- "Boym's reflective nostalgia, exactly: the meme does not want to *return* to the Soviet poster it pastiches, only to dwell next to it for a moment, the way one dwells next to a grave."
