# Historical / Evolutionary lens

## Definition

The historical / evolutionary lens locates a meme in its lineage. It treats the meme not as a single image but as a *family* of variations around a template — what Limor Shifman calls "memes" as distinct from "virals" (a viral is one artifact going wide; a meme is a template under continuous mutation). The lens asks: where did this template originate, what mutations has it accumulated, which variants stuck, what is its current status (live, dormant, archived, ironic, post-ironic), and how does that lineage shape what this particular instance can mean.

## Essential moves

- Name the **origin moment** if knowable — the post, image, scene, or stock photo it descends from — and the date or rough era.
- Identify the **template** (what's fixed, what's variable) and the **mutation tree** (what variants exist; how this instance sits in that tree).
- Mark **diffusion phase**: ascending, peak, decay, revival, ironic afterlife.
- Distinguish **memes from virals** (Shifman) — a meme is the family, not the artifact.
- Note **provenance documentation**, especially KnowYourMeme entries, as primary source for evolution timeline.
- Place the meme in the wider **memetic ecology** it inherits from: image-macro era, reaction-image era, vertical-video era, AI-image era.
- Where relevant, gesture at **Dawkins's** etymology (a sentence, not a paragraph — the original meaning is mostly a curio now).

## Key theorists and short illustrative quotes

- **Limor Shifman**, *Memes in Digital Culture* (MIT Press, 2014): "A meme is a group of digital items sharing common characteristics of content, form, and/or stance, which were created with awareness of each other, and were circulated, imitated, and/or transformed via the Internet by many users."
- **Ryan Milner**, *The World Made Meme* (MIT Press, 2016): on memes as *vernacular creativity*, polyvocal and recombinant by design.
- **An Xiao Mina**, *Memes to Movements* (Beacon, 2019): on memes as the protest infrastructure of diasporic and marginalized publics.
- **Richard Dawkins**, *The Selfish Gene* (1976), ch. 11: the etymological origin of "meme" as a unit of cultural transmission analogous to a gene.

## The Selfish Gene

*The Selfish Gene* is a 1976 science book by evolutionary biologist **Richard Dawkins**. It popularized a gene-centered view of evolution, arguing that natural selection acts primarily at the level of genes rather than individuals or species. The book profoundly shaped public understanding of evolution and introduced concepts that entered both scientific and popular discourse.

### Key facts

* **Author:** Richard Dawkins
* **Publication year:** 1976
* **Publisher:** Oxford University Press
* **Central idea:** Gene-centered view of evolution
* **Famous concept:** "Meme" as a cultural replicator

### Core idea and argument

Dawkins proposed that organisms exist as vehicles for genes, which behave "selfishly" in seeking to propagate themselves. By framing evolution in terms of gene survival, he unified diverse biological phenomena—such as altruism, cooperation, and kin selection—under a single explanatory model. This perspective extended the logic of Charles Darwin’s natural selection into molecular and behavioral domains.

### The concept of the “meme”

In the book’s final chapter, Dawkins introduced the term “meme” to describe a unit of cultural transmission analogous to a gene. Memes—ideas, tunes, or fashions—replicate through imitation and selection, forming the basis for later studies in memetics and cultural evolution. The concept became widely referenced beyond biology, especially in digital culture.

### Influence and reception

*The Selfish Gene* was acclaimed for its clarity and provocative ideas, though it also sparked controversy for its metaphorical use of “selfishness.” The work has sold over a million copies and influenced subsequent books such as **The Extended Phenotype**. It remains a cornerstone text in evolutionary biology and science communication, credited with reframing how both scientists and the public think about evolution.

## Memes in Digital Culture

**Memes in Digital Culture** is a 2013 scholarly book by media researcher **Limor Shifman**, published by the MIT Press. It provides one of the first systematic frameworks for understanding internet memes as cultural and communicative phenomena, blending humor studies, media theory, and participatory culture research.

### Key facts

* **Author:** Limor Shifman
* **Published:** 2013
* **Publisher:** MIT Press
* **Subject:** Internet culture and communication
* **Series:** MIT Press Essential Knowledge series

### Concept and Scope

Shifman defines memes as “groups of digital items sharing common characteristics” that are created with awareness of one another and circulated online. The book distinguishes between memes as cultural artifacts and “viral” content, focusing on collective agency in digital communication rather than individual virality. It situates memes within broader theories of imitation and remix culture.

### Cultural Analysis

Drawing examples from popular platforms like YouTube and Twitter, Shifman analyzes genres such as image macros, political memes, and video parodies. She argues that memes function as tools of social participation, offering users a means to express identity, commentary, and critique within digital publics.

### Scholarly Impact

The book has become a foundational text in internet and media studies, frequently cited for its clear typology and accessible theoretical grounding. It bridges academic and popular perspectives, influencing discussions on digital communication, political discourse, and participatory culture in the age of networked media.

### Reception and Legacy

Critics praise *Memes in Digital Culture* for its balance of rigor and readability, calling it an essential introduction to meme scholarship. It remains widely taught in media and communication studies programs and continues to inform research on online expression, digital folklore, and the politics of humor.

## The Archaeology of Knowledge

*The Archaeology of Knowledge* (French: *L’archéologie du savoir*) is a 1969 work by French philosopher and historian **Michel Foucault**. It sets out his methodological framework for analyzing the history of ideas, especially as developed in his earlier works on science, medicine, and the human sciences.

### Key facts

* **Author:** Michel Foucault
* **Original title:** *L’archéologie du savoir*
* **Publication year:** 1969 (French); 1972 (English translation)
* **Genre:** Philosophy / Historiography
* **Central concept:** Archaeology of discourse

### Context and Purpose

The book represents a methodological turning point for Foucault. It clarifies the principles behind his earlier historical studies, such as *The Birth of the Clinic* and *The Order of Things*. Rather than tracing the evolution of ideas through individual authors or institutions, Foucault emphasizes the **discursive formations** that define what can be thought and said within a historical period.

### Core Concepts

Foucault’s “archaeology” examines the rules governing the production of knowledge. He replaces traditional intellectual history with an analysis of **statements** (*énoncés*) and the systems that structure them. These systems—what he calls “archives”—determine how disciplines like medicine, linguistics, or economics come to define truth and meaning. The book thereby critiques assumptions about continuity, authorship, and progress in intellectual history.

### Influence and Reception

*The Archaeology of Knowledge* was initially challenging for many readers, given its abstract and self-referential style. However, it became a foundational text in critical theory, post-structuralism, and discourse analysis. It influenced scholars in history, cultural studies, and literary theory by shifting focus from ideas to the underlying **conditions of possibility** for knowledge itself.

### Legacy

The work stands as a methodological bridge in Foucault’s thought, connecting his “archaeological” phase to his later “genealogical” studies of power and subjectivity, such as *Discipline and Punish* and *The History of Sexuality*. It remains a key text for understanding how discourse structures human understanding across time.

## The Location of Culture

*The Location of Culture* (1994) is a foundational work of postcolonial theory by Homi K. Bhabha, published by Routledge. It redefines concepts of cultural identity, colonial discourse, and hybridity, introducing key theoretical terms that have shaped contemporary cultural and literary criticism.

### Key facts

* **Author:** Homi K. Bhabha
* **First published:** 1994 (Routledge Classics series)
* **Length:** Approx. 408–440 pages
* **Language:** English
* **Genre:** Literary and cultural theory

### Background and context

Bhabha’s book emerged during the rise of postcolonial studies in the early 1990s, following the work of theorists such as Edward Said and Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak. Drawing on psychoanalysis, structuralism, and postmodern thought, Bhabha investigates how colonial encounters disrupt notions of fixed identity and cultural purity. His analysis positions the colonized and colonizer within dynamic relationships of power and representation.

### Central ideas

At the heart of *The Location of Culture* is Bhabha’s theory of **cultural hybridity**, which describes how new cultural forms emerge from the interstitial “third space” between cultures. He elaborates concepts such as **mimicry**, **ambivalence**, and **liminality** to explain how colonial discourse both enforces and undermines authority. Through essays like “Of Mimicry and Man” and “DissemiNation,” Bhabha shows how hybrid identities destabilize nationalist and imperial narratives. ([Routledge][1])

### Influence and reception

The book has had wide influence across literary criticism, cultural studies, anthropology, and political theory. It helped define postcolonialism’s intellectual vocabulary and remains central in discussions of globalization, migration, and multiculturalism. Though sometimes criticized for dense prose and abstract argumentation, Bhabha’s work is regarded as one of the most significant theoretical contributions to understanding cultural difference and identity formation in the postcolonial world. ## Diaspora and movement (screen 1 introduction)

### The Age of Migration

*The Age of Migration* (Stephen Castles, Hein de Haas, Mark J. Miller; first published 1993, 6th ed. 2020, Guilford Press) is a core migration-studies text on how global mobility reshapes societies, states, and inequality. It links labor migration, forced displacement, transnationalism, and integration to broader economic and political change — treating migration as both cause and consequence of globalization.

For this corpus and screen 1: diasporic memes are cultural production in motion. Templates cross borders, mutate in transit, and name what displacement feels like to the publics that make and circulate them. Castles, de Haas, and Miller supply the frame — migration as structural condition, not anecdote — within which meme lineages and hybrid cultural forms (see Bhabha above) can be read historically.
